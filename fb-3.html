Financial Trading Biases

Behavioral finance explains how psychology, emotions, fear, greed, and cognitive shortcuts influence financial markets and investment decisions. These biases often lead investors to buy too high, sell too early, hold losers too long, and panic during crashes.

Major Trading Biases

Psychology drives markets as much as economics.

1. Overconfidence Bias

Traders believe they are smarter than the market and overestimate their prediction ability.

  • Excessive trading
  • Ignoring risk management
  • Oversized positions

Example: A trader profits during a bull market and starts using leverage aggressively.

2. Loss Aversion

Losses feel emotionally stronger than gains of equal size.

  • Holding losers too long
  • Selling winners too early

Example: Refusing to sell a stock after it falls from ₹500 to ₹350.

3. Herd Mentality

Following the crowd instead of independent analysis.

  • FOMO investing
  • Bubble creation
  • Panic crashes

Example: Buying meme stocks because everyone online is buying them.

4. Confirmation Bias

Seeking information that supports existing beliefs while ignoring risks.

Example: Watching only bullish YouTube videos about a stock already owned.

5. Anchoring Bias

Relying too heavily on initial information such as purchase price.

Example: “I’ll sell once the stock returns to ₹1,000.”

6. Recency Bias

Assuming recent market behavior will continue forever.

Example: Believing markets always recover quickly after several months of gains.

7. Availability Bias

Decisions are based on information easiest to recall.

Example: Assuming all AI stocks are great investments because they dominate headlines.

8. Fear of Missing Out (FOMO)

Buying because others are making money.

Example: Entering a stock after an 80% rally near the top.

9. Regret Aversion

Avoiding decisions due to fear of future regret.

Example: Not buying a strong company because “what if it falls?”

10. Endowment Bias

Overvaluing assets simply because you own them.

Example: Refusing to sell inherited shares despite weak fundamentals.

11. Mental Accounting

Treating money differently depending on its source.

Example: Using trading profits for reckless bets.

12. Gambler’s Fallacy

Believing random events influence future outcomes.

Example: “This stock fell 7 days straight, so tomorrow it must rise.”

13. Self-Attribution Bias

Taking credit for success while blaming failures externally.

Example: Profits = skill, losses = manipulation.

14. Status Quo Bias

Preferring things to remain unchanged.

Example: Holding outdated companies because changing feels uncomfortable.

15. Hindsight Bias

Believing events were predictable after they happened.

Example: “I knew the crash would happen.”

16. Sunk Cost Fallacy

Continuing because money has already been invested.

Example: Averaging down endlessly into a failing company.

17. Optimism Bias

Assuming positive outcomes are more likely than reality.

Example: Believing markets always recover rapidly.

18. Panic Selling

Emotionally selling during crashes.

Example: Selling during the COVID crash near the bottom.

19. Survivorship Bias

Focusing only on successful examples.

Example: Seeing only successful traders on social media.

20. Authority Bias

Blindly trusting famous investors or influencers.

Example: Buying a stock because a celebrity mentioned it.

Why These Biases Matter

Most investing mistakes are not caused by lack of intelligence. They happen because emotions, ego, fear, greed, and psychological shortcuts interfere with rational decision-making. Successful investing is often more about discipline, systems, and emotional control than prediction.
