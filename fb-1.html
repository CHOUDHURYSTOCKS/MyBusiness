Understanding Behavioural Finance

Behavioural Finance studies how emotions, cognitive errors, social influence, and psychological biases affect financial decisions.

Explore Types

Main Types of Behavioural Finance

Psychology + Finance + Human Behaviour

1. Cognitive Behavioural Finance

Focuses on mental errors and thinking mistakes that affect decisions.

  • Overconfidence Bias
  • Anchoring Bias
  • Confirmation Bias
  • Hindsight Bias
Example: An investor keeps buying a falling stock because they are anchored to its old high price.

2. Emotional Behavioural Finance

Studies decisions driven by fear, greed, regret, and emotions.

  • Fear & Greed
  • Loss Aversion
  • Regret Aversion
  • Endowment Effect
Example: Ravi refuses to sell a stock at ₹700 because he fears realizing the loss.

3. Social Behavioural Finance

Examines how society and group behaviour influence investments.

  • Herd Behaviour
  • Market Bubbles
  • Social Influence
  • Peer Pressure
Example: People buy cryptocurrency simply because everyone else is investing.

4. Heuristic-Driven Behaviour

Heuristics are mental shortcuts used for quick decisions.

  • Availability Heuristic
  • Representativeness
  • Rule-of-thumb Investing
Example: Investing in a company because it is popular in the news.

5. Prospect Theory

Developed by Daniel Kahneman and Amos Tversky.

  • Losses hurt more than gains
  • Risk-averse in gains
  • Risk-seeking in losses
Example: Choosing guaranteed ₹5,000 profit instead of a risky ₹10,000 opportunity.

6. Market Behavioural Finance

Studies how collective investor behaviour affects financial markets.

  • Stock Market Bubbles
  • Crashes
  • Excess Volatility
  • Market Anomalies
Example: Panic selling during financial crises causing market crashes.

Real-Life Examples

Understanding behavioural finance through simple situations.

Overconfidence Bias

A trader earns profit twice and believes they can never fail. They invest all their savings in risky stocks and later lose heavily.

Loss Aversion

Investors hold losing stocks too long because accepting losses feels painful emotionally.

Herd Mentality

People invest in trending assets because everyone on social media appears to be making money.

Availability Heuristic

Investors trust companies constantly mentioned in the news without doing proper research.

Risk Behaviour

People usually prefer guaranteed profits but take risky decisions when facing losses.

Market Bubble

Excess optimism drives prices unrealistically high until panic selling causes a crash.
