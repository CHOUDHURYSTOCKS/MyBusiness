Financial Biases

Financial biases are systematic thinking errors and emotional tendencies that affect how people make money decisions. These psychological patterns often lead investors and consumers to act irrationally — even when they believe they are making logical choices.

Major Financial Biases

1. Overconfidence Bias

People believe they know more than they actually do.

Example: Rahul earns profit from a few stock trades and assumes he can “beat the market,” eventually risking all his savings.

2. Loss Aversion

Losses hurt more emotionally than gains feel rewarding.

Example: Priya refuses to sell falling shares because she doesn’t want to “accept” the loss.

3. Anchoring Bias

People rely heavily on the first information they see.

Example: A ₹80,000 phone discounted to ₹55,000 appears cheap even when it is still expensive.

4. Herd Mentality

People follow the crowd instead of thinking independently.

Example: Arjun invests in crypto because “everyone is doing it.”

5. Confirmation Bias

People search only for information supporting their beliefs.

Example: Neha ignores negative news about a company she loves.

6. Recency Bias

People assume recent trends will continue forever.

Example: Investors think markets only go up after a long bull run.

7. Availability Bias

People judge probability using easily remembered information.

Example: Someone avoids flying after hearing about airplane accidents.

8. Status Quo Bias

People prefer things to remain unchanged.

Example: Keeping money in a low-interest account for years.

9. Mental Accounting

People treat money differently depending on its source.

Example: Spending bonus money carelessly but protecting salary income.

10. Sunk Cost Fallacy

Continuing because too much has already been invested.

Example: Funding a failing business because ₹10 lakh was already spent.

11. Endowment Effect

People overvalue things simply because they own them.

Example: Emotional attachment increases the perceived value of a car.

12. Framing Bias

Decisions change depending on how information is presented.

Example: “90% success rate” sounds better than “10% failure rate.”

13. Present Bias

People prefer instant rewards over future benefits.

Example: Spending on shopping instead of long-term investing.

14. Optimism Bias

People underestimate risks to themselves.

Example: Ignoring health insurance because “nothing bad will happen.”

15. Regret Aversion

Fear of future regret blocks decision-making.

Example: Avoiding stocks forever after one bad investment experience.

16. Self-Attribution Bias

Success is credited to skill while failures are blamed externally.

17. Gambler’s Fallacy

Believing past random events influence future outcomes.

Example: Assuming a falling stock must recover soon.

18. Home Bias

Investors prefer familiar local markets.

Example: Investing only in Indian stocks without diversification.

19. Survivorship Bias

Focusing only on successful examples while ignoring failures.

20. Authority Bias

Trusting experts or celebrities too much.

Example: Buying stocks just because a famous investor recommended them.

How to Reduce Financial Biases

Use Written Rules

Create clear investment principles before making decisions.

Think Statistically

Focus on long-term probability rather than emotions.

Seek Opposing Views

Challenge your assumptions and avoid echo chambers.

Delay Big Decisions

Avoid emotional investing and impulsive purchases.

Automate Investing

SIPs and automatic savings reduce emotional reactions.

Review Mistakes

Maintain a financial journal and learn objectively.
